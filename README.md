# Short-tail Stingray Site Fidelity & Occurrence — Gansbaai, South Africa
 
Long-term site fidelity and environmental drivers of short-tail stingray (*Bathytoshia brevicaudata*) occurrence in South Africa: evidence from decade-scale sightings data and photo-identification.
 
**Authors:** R. G. A. Watson¹ & A. J. Webber²
¹ Department of Biological Sciences, University of Cape Town, South Africa
² Dyer Island Conservation Trust, Gansbaai, South Africa

## Overview

This repository contains the analysis code and supporting materials for a study characterising occurrence patterns, individual residency, and local abundance of the short-tail stingray at a shark-cage-diving aggregation site (Joubertsdam) near Gansbaai, Western Cape, South Africa. The project draws on an 11-year dataset (January 2014–December 2024) comprising 5,730 standardised boat-based trips and a parallel photo-identification archive, representing the first population-level assessment of this species in South Africa and the longest continuous observational record of the species reported globally.
 
## Background
 
Large coastal elasmobranchs are among the most data-deficient marine vertebrate groups, and batoids in particular remain underrepresented in photo-identification and mark-recapture research relative to sharks. This project addresses that gap for *B. brevicaudata*, a species whose site fidelity and population structure remain poorly characterised across most of its Southern Hemisphere range, by combining:
 
- **Sightings-based occurrence modelling** — relating monthly relative sightings to environmental covariates.
- **Photographic identification** — using stable dorsal spot patterns to individually recognise stingrays over time.
- **Open-population mark-recapture** — estimating apparent survival, detection probability, and super-population size.

## Data
 
- **Sightings data:** Standardised per-trip records (2014–2024), including sea surface temperature (SST), depth, date, and stingray counts.
- **Photo-identification catalogue:** Individual encounter histories derived from dorsal spot-pattern matching, covering 11 identified individuals across 398 positive identifications.
Raw data are not included in this repository due to file size and data-sharing agreements but can be made available upon reasonable request to the corresponding author.
